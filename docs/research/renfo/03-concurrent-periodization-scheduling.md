# Concurrent Training & Scheduling Strength ("Renfo") Alongside Endurance Running

**Purpose:** Evidence base and codable decision rules for a deterministic engine that places strength sessions within a runner's week and season. Targets recreational-to-competitive distance/trail runners.

**Date:** 2026-06-16
**Scope:** Interference effect, intra-day scheduling, weekly scheduling, seasonal sync, taper, minimal effective dose, fatigue gating. Plus a critique of Vorcelab's current fusion algo with concrete fixes.

> Evidence-quality note: items below are tagged **[A]** strong (meta-analysis / RCT consensus), **[B]** moderate (single study or mixed evidence), **[C]** practitioner heuristic (reputable coaches, mechanistically plausible, weaker direct evidence). Build hard gates on **[A]**, soft defaults on **[B]/[C]**.

---

## 1. The Interference Effect

### What it is
First described by **Hickson (1980)**: adding endurance training to a strength program blunted strength/power gains (esp. after ~week 8-10), while strength training did not blunt endurance gains to the same degree. The interference is **asymmetric**: endurance compromises strength/power more than strength compromises endurance. For runners this is good news — strength rarely hurts running adaptation and usually helps (running economy). The risk we manage is the *reverse*: poorly placed endurance load (and accumulated fatigue) degrading the quality and adaptation of strength sessions, and DOMS from strength degrading key runs.

### Magnitude (meta-analytic) **[A]**
- Wilson et al. meta-analysis (concurrent vs. isolated): strength dev. ES ≈ **1.44** concurrent vs **1.76** strength-only; hypertrophy ES ≈ **0.85** vs **1.23**; power ES ≈ **0.55** vs **0.91**. Power is hit hardest. Running-mode endurance produced larger decrements than cycling. ([ResearchGate / Wilson 2012](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/51719597_Concurrent_Training_A_Meta-Analysis_Examining_Interference_of_Aerobic_and_Resistance_Exercises))
- Schumann et al. 2021 (Sports Medicine systematic review): interference on lower-body strength appears in **trained** athletes; in trained subjects effect is stronger when strength + endurance are in the **same session** vs separated by **6 or 24 h**. ([Springer s40279-021-01426-9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-021-01426-9))
- Same-session vs separated meta-data: trained lower-body 1RM ES ≈ **−0.35** (p<0.01) overall; **same session (<20 min apart) ES ≈ −0.66**; **separated >2 h ES ≈ −0.10 (n.s.)**. Moderately trained ≈ −0.20 (n.s.); untrained ≈ +0.03 (no interference). ([PMC8053170](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053170))

### Mechanism **[B]**
Acute molecular conflict: resistance exercise activates **mTORC1** (anabolic; elevated up to ~18 h post). Endurance exercise — especially glycogen-depleting / high-intensity — activates **AMPK** (cellular energy sensor), which can suppress mTORC1. The "AMPK–mTOR" hypothesis is the leading but incomplete explanation; chronic fatigue, residual glycogen depletion, and overreaching also contribute. ([GSSI SSE-136](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-136-using-nutrition-and-molecular-biology-to-maximize-concurrent-training), [TrainingPeaks](https://www.trainingpeaks.com/blog/risks-of-concurrent-training/))

### Who it affects / how to minimize
- **More affected:** trained/competitive runners, high endurance frequency+intensity, same-session stacking, power/explosive goals. **Less affected:** beginners (often see no interference). ([PMC8053170](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053170))
- **Minimize by:** (1) separate strength and *key* endurance by **≥6 h** (≥3 h is the practical minimum, >6 h better) ([PMC8053170](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053170)); (2) avoid stacking heavy lower-body strength immediately before/after glycogen-depleting runs; (3) refuel carbohydrate between sessions to lower AMPK drive ([GSSI](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-136-using-nutrition-and-molecular-biology-to-maximize-concurrent-training)); (4) cap heavy lower-body frequency; (5) prioritize *running* as the target adaptation for runners and treat strength as a supportive stimulus.

### The upside (why we still prescribe strength) **[A]**
Heavy resistance training (≥80%, ideally ≥90% 1RM) improves **running economy** (pooled g ≈ **−0.32**, i.e. better economy) and **time-trial performance** (g ≈ **−0.24**), outperforming plyometrics for economy. Typical effective protocol: ~2×/week, ~3×4-10RM on a few multi-joint leg exercises, 8-12+ weeks. ([Heavy RT vs plyo meta, PMC9653533](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9653533/); [Middle/long-distance economy meta, PMC11052887](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11052887/); [Rønnestad female endurance RCT, PLOS One](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0150799))

## 2. Intra-Day Scheduling (same-day rules)

### Minimum spacing **[A]**
- If strength and a **key** endurance session share a day, separate by **≥6 h** (hard preference). **≥3 h** is the acceptable floor; <3 h / same-session is the interference-maximizing case in trained runners. ([PMC8053170](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8053170), [Schumann 2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-021-01426-9))

### Session order (when same session is unavoidable) **[A/B]**
- Intra-session **order has negligible effect on endurance, small effect on strength/power**; doing the *second* exercise mode fatigued blunts it slightly. ([Intra-session sequence meta, PMC5752732](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5752732/); [Eddens 2018, PMC7224562](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7224562/))
- **Rule:** put the day's *priority* adaptation first while fresh.
  - Goal = preserve **strength/power** quality → **strength first**, run after.
  - Goal = key **quality run** (intervals/threshold/long) → **run first**, strength after (and keep strength lighter/upper-body).

### Morning vs evening **[C]**
- No strong chronobiology mandate. Practical levers: (1) refuel carbs between AM and PM sessions to blunt AMPK ([GSSI](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-136-using-nutrition-and-molecular-biology-to-maximize-concurrent-training)); (2) avoid glycogen-depleting AM run immediately before PM strength without refeed; (3) strength after a *low-intensity, non-depleting* easy run can actually enhance endurance signaling. ([GSSI](https://www.gssiweb.org/sports-science-exchange/article/sse-136-using-nutrition-and-molecular-biology-to-maximize-concurrent-training))

### Codable intra-day logic
```
if strength_day == key_endurance_day:
    if gap_hours >= 6:        ok = true                # preferred
    elif gap_hours >= 3:      ok = true; flag="suboptimal_spacing"
    else:                     # forced same-session
        order = (priority == "strength") ? [strength, run] : [run, strength]
        if priority == "run": downgrade strength to light/upper-body
        require_refeed = (run is glycogen_depleting)   # carbs between
```

## 3. Weekly Scheduling

### Seiler "hard days hard, easy days easy" / polarized **[A for ID, C for strength placement]**
Polarized intensity distribution (~80% easy / ~20% hard, minimal "gray zone") is well supported for endurance. The corollary for strength placement: **don't create a gray-zone day.** Two valid philosophies — pick one per athlete and be consistent:

1. **Stack strength on hard/quality days** (keeps easy days truly easy and protects recovery days). Heavy/strenuous strength goes *after* the quality run, same day. Pro: protects easy-day recovery; con: very demanding hard days, needs good fueling/sleep.
2. **Strength on dedicated/easy days** (separate stimulus, better strength quality). Pro: fresher strength sessions; con: risks adding load to "easy" days — keep the run that day genuinely easy/short and lift hard, or take the run off.
([Seiler/polarized overview](https://www.fasttalklabs.com/pathways/polarized-training/); [Marathon 80/20](https://marathonireland.com/blog/polarized-training-8020/); PT discussion of hard-vs-easy day strength: [The Mother Runners](https://www.themotherrunners.com/should-runners-strength-train-on-hard-or-easy-days-a-physical-therapists-take/))

### Hard rules (gates) **[A/C]**
- **Never schedule heavy lower-body strength the day before** a long run, a key quality session, or a race. DOMS/neuromuscular fatigue degrades those runs. ([Healthhp scheduling](https://www.healthhp.com.au/post/strength-training-for-runners-scheduling-tips-for-maximum-benefit), [Runna](https://support.runna.com/en/articles/6830469-when-to-complete-your-strength-sessions))
- **Cap heavy lower-body strength to 1-2×/week** (1× in-season maintenance is sufficient — see §6).
- **Heavy lower-body strength → allow ~48 h before next high-quality run** on the same musculature (DOMS window).

### Day-placement heuristic (typical 1 quality + 1 long week)
```
Mon  easy run            -> OPTIONAL heavy strength (best slot: far from quality & long)
Tue  QUALITY run         -> strength only if same-day AFTER run (else none)
Wed  easy/recovery       -> no heavy legs (within 48h of Tue, day before nothing key)
Thu  easy or 2nd quality -> optional 2nd (lighter) strength after run
Fri  easy/rest           -> NO heavy legs (eve of long run)  <-- gate
Sat  LONG run            -> no heavy legs before; light core/upper OK after
Sun  rest/easy
```
Single-quality-day weeks: best heavy-strength slot = **early week, ≥48 h before next quality, never eve of long run.**

## 4. Seasonal Synchronization

### Strength block progression (classic linear/block) **[B]**
Anatomical adaptation → Maximal strength → Power/conversion → Maintenance. Map onto run phases:

| Run phase | Strength focus (DUP/block) | Load/intent | Heavy-leg freq |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base / general prep** | Anatomical adaptation → **max strength** (force) | 70-90% 1RM, 3-6 reps, multi-joint | 2×/wk |
| **Build** | Max strength → early **power**; add running-specific volume tolerance | mixed heavy + moderate | 1-2×/wk |
| **Specific / sharpening** | **Power / explosive** (RFD), plyometrics, low volume | ≥90% 1RM low-rep + fast/plyo | 1×/wk |
| **Taper / race** | **Maintenance → deload** (see §5) | reduce volume, keep intensity/intent | 0-1× light |

### Block vs DUP **[A]**
- Block (2-4 wk focused phases) and **Daily Undulating Periodization** (varying focus within the week) produce **similar strength outcomes**; block trended slightly better for max strength / RFD in T&F athletes but not significantly. ([Block vs DUP T&F, PubMed 22173008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22173008/); [Stronger by Science periodization](https://www.strongerbyscience.com/periodization-data/))
- **Recommendation for runners:** Use **block emphasis across the macrocycle** (anatomical→max→power→maintenance synced to run phases) but allow **light intra-week undulation** (e.g., one heavier + one lighter/explosive session in higher-volume weeks). DUP is attractive for variety and for athletes with limited blocks of access. ([Laura Norris on periodization for runners](https://lauranorrisrunning.com/periodization-training-undulating-linear/))

## 5. Taper

### Stop heavy strength **[B/C]**
- **Stop heavy/high-CNS strength ~1-3 weeks out** depending on event demands. Common practitioner guidance: cease heavy lifting as the running taper begins (~2-3 wk for marathon). ([Outside Online — strength taper research](https://www.outsideonline.com/health/training-performance/strength-training-taper-research/); [RunnersConnect](https://runnersconnect.net/when-should-i-stop-strength-training-at-the-gym-before-the-marathon/); [Central Performance](https://centralperformance.com.au/blog/how-to-taper-your-strength-for-optimal-performance))
- **Strength gains persist 2-3+ weeks** without training (detraining is slow for strength), so stopping heavy work 1-3 wk out does **not** lose the adaptation. ([Outside Online](https://www.outsideonline.com/health/training-performance/strength-training-taper-research/))

### Distance/terrain modulation **[C]** (synthesis — codify as defaults, not hard evidence)
- **Short road (5-10k):** last heavy session ~**7-10 days** out; keep brief explosive/neuromuscular primer ~**3-4 days** out (low volume, high intent) to preserve RFD.
- **Half / Marathon (road):** last heavy session ~**10-14 days** out; light maintenance (2× short, 30-40% load or bodyweight, low volume) through taper; **no gym final 5 days.**
- **Trail / ultra (eccentric demand high):** stop heavy **eccentric/lower-body ~14-21 days** out to fully clear DOMS and muscle damage (downhill running adds eccentric load); keep light mobility/stability only in final week.

### What to keep **[C]**
- Final 1-2 wk: **maintenance dose** — 1-2 short sessions, reduce volume, **preserve intensity/intent** on a few reps. Optional explosive primer **3-4 days** pre-race. **No new exercises, no DOMS-inducing loads.** ([Planted Runner taper](https://theplantedrunner.com/the-exact-marathon-taper-plan-for-fresh-legs-on-race-day/), [Point to Point](https://www.pointtopointstrength.com/articles/how-to-strength-train-the-week-before-a-race))

## 6. Minimal Effective Dose & Real Constraints (1×/week gym)

### Maintenance dosing **[A/B]**
- Strength is **maintained with as little as 1 session/week, ~1 set/exercise, IF relative intensity (load) is preserved.** Volume can drop sharply; intensity cannot. ([Run Weekly MED](https://www.runweekly.com/the-minimum-effective-dose-of-strength-training-for-runners/); [Trail Runner minimal-dose](https://www.trailrunnermag.com/training/trail-tips-training/minimal-dose-resistance-training/))
- ~**30-60 min/week** of strength was sufficient to improve maximal running performance in recreational runners. ([Minimal-dose RCT, PubMed 28462571](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28462571/))

### Prioritizing the single weekly slot
```
Single weekly strength slot -> spend it on HEAVY, MULTI-JOINT, LOWER-BODY (max-strength / economy driver):
  e.g. squat / trap-bar deadlift / split squat / calf, 3-4 exercises, 3x4-6 @ heavy
Placement: early-mid week, >=48h from a key run, NEVER eve of long run/quality/race.
Phase intent on that single slot still follows season:
  base->max strength | specific->add 1 explosive movement | taper->lighten or skip
Supplement off-gym days with bodyweight/plyo/core that need no equipment (low fatigue).
```
Rationale: with one slot, max-strength stimulus on prime movers yields the running-economy benefit (§1 upside) at the lowest interference cost; accessory/mobility goes to no-equipment home days.

## 7. Recovery / Fatigue Gating (when to SKIP, lighten, or substitute)

### Signals & thresholds **[A for ACWR/TSB ranges; B/C for DOMS gating]**
- **ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio):** sweet spot **0.8-1.3**; **≥1.5 = elevated injury risk.** When combined run+strength load pushes ACWR high, **do not add heavy strength** that week — lighten or substitute. (Use as a soft flag, not gospel — ACWR has methodological critiques.) ([Science for Sport](https://www.scienceforsport.com/acutechronic-workload-ratio/); [Springer ACWR review s40279-020-01308-6](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-020-01308-6))
- **TSB / form:** very negative TSB (deep fatigue) → skip/lighten heavy strength; note elevated injury risk also reported at extreme *positive* swings. ([Science for Sport](https://www.scienceforsport.com/acutechronic-workload-ratio/))
- **DOMS:** active lower-body DOMS (esp. before/around a key run) → **skip heavy legs**, substitute mobility/upper-body/easy.
- **Post-race:** muscle damage (esp. marathon/trail/ultra) persists days-weeks → **light only** until cleared.

### Codable gating logic
```
function strength_gate(day):
    if eve_of(long_run | quality | race):            return SKIP_LOWER_HEAVY  # §3 gate
    if active_lower_DOMS or RPE_residual_high:        return SUB_MOBILITY
    if days_since_hard_race < race_recovery_window:   return LIGHT_ONLY
        # window: 5-10k ~3-5d, half ~7d, marathon ~10-14d, trail/ultra ~14-21d
    if ACWR >= 1.5 or TSB very_negative:              return SKIP_or_LIGHTEN
    if 1.3 <= ACWR < 1.5:                             return LIGHTEN (cut volume, keep intent)
    if sleep_poor or sickness:                        return SUB_MOBILITY
    return PROCEED_per_phase
```
**Substitution ladder when gated:** heavy compound → light/explosive (low volume) → bodyweight/core/stability → mobility/foam-roll → full rest.

## 8. Critique of Vorcelab's Current Fusion Algo & Concrete Fixes

**Current algo (as described):** run phase → DUP focus (base→force, build→volume, specific→puissance, taper/race→deload); caps heavy lower-body to 1×/week; avoids eve of key sessions & race day; light-only in post-race recovery.

### What's right (keep) ✅
- **1×/week heavy-leg cap** — aligns with MED + interference minimization (§1, §6). [A]
- **Avoiding eve of key sessions / race day** — matches the strongest practical gate (§3). [A/C]
- **Light-only post-race** — correct, and should be *duration-scaled* by event (below). [C]
- **Phase→focus mapping** — directionally sound (force in base, power in specific). [B]

### Issues & fixes 🔧

1. **"build → volume" is questionable.** Hypertrophy/volume emphasis in *build* adds the most fatigue exactly when run intensity ramps, and runners don't want mass. **Fix:** build = **max-strength→early power** (heavy, low-volume), reserve any hypertrophy/anatomical-adaptation volume for **early base**. Sequence: base(anatomical→max) → build(max→power) → specific(power/RFD) → taper(maintenance→deload). (§4)

2. **No explicit intra-day ≥6 h spacing rule.** The single biggest evidence-based lever (same-session interference ES −0.66 vs −0.10 when separated) isn't encoded. **Fix:** add hard rule — if strength shares a day with a *key* run, require **≥6 h** gap (≥3 h floor); else force order = priority-first and downgrade the secondary. (§1, §2)

3. **No 48 h post-heavy-legs DOMS shadow.** "Avoid eve of key session" prevents *before* but not residual DOMS *after*. **Fix:** after heavy lower-body, block high-quality runs on that musculature for **~48 h**. (§3)

4. **Taper is single-mode ("deload").** Real taper depends on **event distance + terrain.** **Fix:** parameterize last-heavy-session offset: short road ~7-10 d, half/marathon ~10-14 d, trail/ultra ~14-21 d; keep a maintenance/explosive primer (low volume, high intent) ~3-4 d out for road; mobility-only final week. (§5)

5. **Post-race "light-only" not duration-scaled.** **Fix:** recovery window by event — 5-10k ~3-5 d, half ~7 d, marathon ~10-14 d, trail/ultra ~14-21 d before resuming heavy. (§5, §7)

6. **No fatigue/readiness gating beyond calendar.** **Fix:** add ACWR/TSB/DOMS gate that can **skip or lighten** the scheduled strength even on a "legal" day: ACWR ≥1.5 or very-negative TSB → skip/lighten; 1.3-1.5 → cut volume keep intent; active DOMS/poor sleep/sickness → substitute mobility. (§6, §7)

7. **No "single gym slot" prioritization mode.** **Fix:** when access = 1×/week, force that slot to **heavy multi-joint lower-body** (economy driver) at the best calendar position, and route accessory/plyo/core to no-equipment home days. (§6)

8. **No carb-refeed / glycogen flag for same-day stacking.** **Fix (soft):** if a glycogen-depleting run precedes same-day strength, flag `require_refeed` to blunt AMPK suppression. (§1, §2)

### Suggested deterministic pipeline (priority-ordered)
```
1. Gate (hard skips):  eve_of_key | race_day | active_DOMS | post_race_window  -> SKIP/LIGHT/SUB
2. Readiness gate:     ACWR/TSB/sleep/sickness                                  -> SKIP/LIGHTEN/SUB
3. Phase focus:        base=max-strength | build=max->power | specific=power | taper=maint/deload
4. Dose cap:           heavy_lower_body <= 1-2x/wk (1x in-season; 1x if single-slot)
5. Day placement:      early/mid week, >=48h before next quality, not eve of long/quality/race
6. Intra-day:          if shares key-run day -> require >=6h (>=3h floor) else order=priority-first
7. Taper override:     last_heavy = f(distance, terrain); keep low-vol high-intent primer
8. Substitution ladder when gated: heavy -> light/explosive -> bodyweight/core -> mobility -> rest
```
