# Strength & Neuromuscular Training Science for Trail / Mountain / Ultra Running

> Research brief for Vorcelab's `renfo` (strength) module. Goal: make strength programming **best-in-class and trail/ultra-specific**, not generic road-runner strength.
> Compiled 2026-06-16. URLs inline. Numbers are cited; controversies and weak-evidence points are flagged explicitly.
> Scope note: much of the strongest RCT evidence is on *road/track* runners; trail-specific evidence is dominated by **descriptive fatigue/EIMD studies** (Millet, Vernillo, Giandolini) plus **downhill-running EIMD/RBE** literature. Where we extrapolate from road to trail, it is flagged.

---

## 0. The trail-specific problem statement

Trail/ultra running differs from road running in ways that change strength programming:

- **Eccentric load dominates.** Descending loads the quadriceps (and plantar flexors) eccentrically. Downhill running causes knee-extensor MVC force losses of **−14% to −55%**, plantar-flexor losses **−15% to −25%**, with rate-of-force-development (RFD) impairment persisting **several days**. (Narrative review, *Sports Med* / PMC12592170: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12592170/)
- **Failure mode is peripheral/lower-limb, not systemic.** In a 156 km ultra, **knee-extensor strength fell ~41%** at the finish while **handgrip fell only 2–5%** → fatigue is localized to the legs, so train the legs for damage-resistance, not the whole body for "general fatigue." (*Profiling muscle strength/power over 156 km*, PMC12832751: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12832751/)
- **Durability, not VO₂max, is the late-race differentiator.** Strength training preserves running economy *when fatigued* — the trail-relevant property (see §3).
- **Terrain is unstable.** Ankle/foot/proprioception and trunk stability matter far more than on road.

Implication for the module: prioritize (1) **eccentric/downhill durability**, (2) **maximal strength + heavy-load economy**, (3) **single-leg & posterior-chain**, (4) **proprioception/ankle**, (5) **trunk for time-on-feet/poles/pack** — and periodize so the highest-damage work is **far from** big mountain long runs.

## 1. Eccentric & downhill training (the trail-specific keystone)

### 1.1 The damage signature
- Downhill running EIMD scales with **steeper grade, longer duration, faster speed**. (PMC12592170)
- Classic dose study: **30 min downhill at −15% grade** → **~21% loss of knee-extensor MVC** + **~7% rise in O₂ cost** of subsequent level running, persisting for days. (Trail Runner / running-explained summaries of the Chen/2007-type protocols; review PMC12592170)
- Metabolic cost of *downhill* is **U-shaped**: minimum cost between **−10% and −20%** grade; steeper than that, braking cost rises. (PMC12592170) → training descents in the −10 to −20% band maximizes economy work per unit damage; steeper descents are a *durability/skill* stimulus, not an economy one.

### 1.2 Repeated-Bout Effect (RBE) — the single most actionable trail tool
One bout of damaging eccentric/downhill work protects against the next bout: **less soreness, smaller CK rise, less MVC loss, less drop in voluntary activation.** (PMC12592170)

**Quantified RBE protocol (trained female distance runners, PMC11209549: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11209549/):**

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Grade | **−15%** |
| Speed | **70% of vVO₂peak** (≈8.8 km/h in that cohort) |
| Bout structure | 6 × 5-min alternating level/downhill, 2-min standing rest |
| Bout spacing | **3 weeks apart** |
| 2nd-bout effect | ↓ blood lactate (p=0.005), ↓ soreness in quads/hams/gastroc/glutes (p≤0.001), ↓ RPE (p=0.002), ↓ downhill VO₂ by ~1.6–2.2 mL·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ |

**Protection duration:** measured benefit at **3 weeks**; general EIMD literature suggests protection can persist **up to ~6 months** but this is *unproven for downhill specifically* (flagged as weak evidence). (PMC11209549)

**Race-prep tactic (well supported):** a **single 30-min downhill bout ~1 week before** a hilly race attenuates EIMD symptoms on race day. (Springer narrative review 10.1007/s40279-020-01355-z; running-explained rundown: https://www.runningexplained.com/post/research-rundown-how-to-train-for-downhill-running-reduce-soreness-and-improve-race-day-results)

### 1.3 NEW / precise protocol — isometric preconditioning
A **short isometric preconditioning** session **2 days before** downhill work reduces damage:
- **10 maximal voluntary isometric contractions at LONG muscle length**, 48 h prior.
- Result: strength **returned to baseline by 72 h** (vs no full recovery by 96 h in controls) and **DOMS −25% at 48 h**. (Springer review 10.1007/s40279-020-01355-z)
- *Codable*: offer a "pre-descent primer" — e.g. 10 long-length split-squat isometric holds / Spanish-squat holds, scheduled 2 days before a big descent block.

### 1.4 Programmable downhill progression (synthesized, evidence-anchored)
Start **10–15 min gentle downhill**, build to race-grade/duration over **4–6 weeks**; one prior session reduces DOMS severity by an estimated **~50–80%** for the next **2–6 weeks**. (precisionpt / running-explained syntheses of RBE data — *practitioner estimate, flagged*.)

| Phase | Downhill rep prescription | Eccentric gym analog |
|---|---|---|
| Intro (wk 1–2) | 10–15 min easy continuous downhill, ≤−8% | Tempo eccentrics: 4 s lowering, bodyweight |
| Build (wk 3–5) | 4–6 × 2–4 min @ −10 to −15%, easy effort | Heavy eccentric step-downs, 3–4 s, +load |
| Specific | Repeated descents in −10/−20% band on race terrain | Reduce volume; keep RBE "topped up" every ~2–3 wk |
| Race week | **1 × 30-min downhill bout ~7 days out**, then stop | none |

> **Controversy:** RBE reduces *soreness/CK* more reliably than it reduces *neuromuscular fatigue (MVC loss)*. Athletes can feel "fine" yet still be force-impaired. Don't let reduced DOMS license a hard session too soon. (PMC12592170)

## 2. Uphill power & vertical

### 2.1 Gym strength vs hill reps — what carries over
- **Heavy resistance > plyometrics** for running economy: pooled effect **g=−0.32 (heavy)** vs **g=−0.13 (plyo)**. (Heavy-vs-plyo meta-analysis, PMC9653533 / Springer 10.1186/s40798-022-00511-1)
- Best economy gains from **near-maximal load: ≥90% 1RM or ≤4RM**, over **≥10 weeks**, **2–3×/week**. (PMC9653533; middle/long-distance meta-analysis PMC11052887)
- **Uphill running itself** improves economy and max velocity by optimizing stride mechanics (RCT, *Sci Rep* 10.1038/s41598-025-08275-w) — i.e. **gym builds the force; hills make it specific.** Use both.

### 2.2 Vertical-kilometer (VK) insight
In VK, runners lift the COM far more per step (extra net mechanical work). Top vs low-level VK runners differ mainly in **mechanical power, metabolic power and velocity** — i.e. **power-to-weight on steep grade is trainable and discriminating**. (*Running Economy in the VK*, PMC10708873: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10708873/)

### 2.3 Practical climbing-power tools
- **Hill sprints / short hill reps**: high fiber recruitment, power + economy, low DOMS if kept short (8–12 s, full recovery).
- **Uphill weighted carries / weight-vest hikes** (Uphill Athlete staple): load a pack and climb steep grade — strong specificity for vert + pack carrying (uphillathlete.com; note: their pages intermittently 503).

## 3. Durability / fatigue-resistance (Maunder framework)

**Durability** = "the time of onset and magnitude of deterioration in physiological-profiling characteristics during prolonged exercise" (Maunder et al. 2021). It is the **fourth endurance parameter** alongside VO₂max, threshold, economy. (JAP 2025 10.1152/japplphysiol.00671.2025; review PMC12576026)

### 3.1 The landmark strength→durability RCT (codable gold)
*Strength Training Improves Running Economy Durability and Fatigued High-Intensity Performance*, **MSSE 2025** (PubMed 40016936: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40016936/; OA PDF: https://www.fisiologiadelejercicio.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Strength-Training-Improves-Running-Economy-Durability-and-Fatigued.pdf):
- 28 well-trained males, **maximal strength + plyometric, 2×/week × 10 weeks.**
- Economy measured every 15 min across a **90-min heavy-domain run**.
- **Strength group: economy −2.1% (improved) at 90 min vs control +0.6% (worse).**
- **Time-to-exhaustion (95% VO₂max) after the run: +35% strength vs −8% control.**
- → Strength's biggest payoff is **preserving economy and high-intensity capacity when already fatigued** — exactly the late-race trail scenario. This is the headline citation for the module.

### 3.2 Trail-specific durability evidence
- Higher-level ultra-trail runners show **less neuromuscular fatigue** post-race than amateurs. (MDPI 18/10/5119: https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/18/10/5119)
- In the 156 km study, **non-finishers had ~27% lower baseline strength** than finishers → **baseline strength predicts completion**, even though in-race strength-loss magnitude did *not* predict finish rank. (PMC12832751)

### 3.3 Single-leg & posterior chain (the durability exercises)
- Every stride lands/stabilizes/pushes on **one leg**; ground forces ≈ **2.5–3× bodyweight**. (Strength Running; Canadian Running)
- Prioritize **single-leg**: split squat, rear-foot-elevated split squat, step-up/step-down, **single-leg RDL** (posterior chain + balance), single-leg calf raise.
- Posterior chain (glutes/hamstrings/calves) resists the late-race shift onto over-fatigued quads and protects descents.

## 4. Ankle / foot / proprioception / technical-terrain stability

**Best-evidence number:** proprioceptive/balance training reduces ankle sprains:
- **All athletes: RR 0.65 → 35% risk reduction** (NNT 17)
- **Recurrent sprains: RR 0.64 → 36%** (NNT 13)
- **Primary prevention (no prior sprain): RR 0.57 → 43%** (NNT 33, wide CI — flagged) (Evidence-based review, PMC5737043: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5737043/)

**Protocol parameters that worked (wide but effective ranges):**

| Parameter | Effective range |
|---|---|
| Session length | **5–30 min** |
| Frequency | **1–5×/week** |
| Program length | **4 weeks → full season** |
| Exercises | single-leg balance (eyes closed), wobble-board/DynaDisc, catch-throw tasks |

Takeaway: **even short, equipment-light proprioception dosing works** → easy to embed daily.
Plus: **intrinsic foot strengthening**, single-leg stands, and **specific exposure to uneven/technical terrain** (progress slowly). (thefootstudio; adventure.physio)

## 5. Core / trunk for time-on-feet, poles, pack carrying

- **Trunk/upper-body fatigue worsens running economy** (3-armed RCT, PMC6722636: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6722636/) → core endurance is an *economy* issue over ultra durations, not just aesthetics.
- Core training can improve **endurance and running economy** (PLOS One, via Nike summary).
- **Poles** shift load from legs to upper body and add descent stability — but **don't reliably save energy**; they become **more economical only above ~26% grade** (most ultra climbs are below: Hardrock 25%, Western States Devil's Thumb 19%, Leadville Hope Pass 18%). **No published running-with-poles studies exist** (flagged gap). (Koop/CTS: https://trainright.com/science-trekking-poles-trail-running-ultrarunning/)
- → If athlete races with poles or pack: train **trunk anti-rotation/anti-extension endurance** (planks, Pallof, suitcase/farmer carries) and **pole-specific** upper-body/lat work. Carries double as posterior-chain + grip + trunk endurance.

## 6. Plyometrics for trail (use, but with caution)

- Plyo improves economy but **less than heavy lifting** (g=−0.13 vs −0.32; effective only at **≤12 km/h** speeds). (PMC9653533)
- **Risk is dose-driven:** sudden volume/intensity spikes are the dominant injury predictor; high-amplitude jumps introduced too fast → calf strain, Achilles tendinopathy, knee pain. (Nielsen review via marathonhandbook synthesis)
- **DOMS/recovery:** an acute plyo bout raises DOMS + inflammatory markers and **drops jump performance for up to 72 h**. 3×/week caused an initial performance decrement (acute overreaching). (PMC12515833)
- **Dose ceiling (NSCA):** plyo-only session **3–6 sets × 2–5 reps**; quality > quantity.

> **Trail caveat:** plyometric DOMS *stacks* with downhill EIMD. Never schedule hard plyo into the same micro-window as a big descent block or B2B long runs. Keep plyo **low-volume, high-quality, early in the week, away from damage work.**

## 7. How periodization differs for trail vs road

| Axis | Road runner | Trail/ultra runner |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant load | Concentric, repetitive | **High eccentric** (descents) |
| Failure mode | Central/metabolic | **Peripheral, lower-limb** (KE ~−41%/156 km) |
| Key strength goal | Economy | **Fatigued/durable economy + descent durability** |
| Unique stressors | — | **B2B long runs, multi-hour vert, pole/pack, technical terrain** |
| Extra blocks needed | — | **RBE downhill block; proprioception; eccentric tempo** |

**Phase model (synthesis of McMillan/TrainingPeaks periodization + trail demands):**

1. **Anatomical adaptation / stability (3–4 wk):** bodyweight → light load, high reps, single-leg, proprioception, intrinsic foot. (Uphill Athlete: first 2–3 ME sessions bodyweight-only to avoid severe DOMS.)
2. **Max strength (6–10 wk):** ≥90% 1RM / ≤4RM, **2×/week**, big bilateral + single-leg; this drives economy/durability. (PMC9653533; MSSE 2025)
3. **Conversion → muscular endurance & power:** convert to ME (Uphill Athlete weighted-step/box ME 1×/wk), add **low-volume plyo + hill power**, **introduce structured downhill/RBE**.
4. **Specific:** race-terrain descents, B2B long runs, "top-up" RBE every **2–3 weeks**; gym volume drops, intensity (neural) maintained.
5. **Taper:** keep load, cut volume.

## 8. Practical weekly programming & what to AVOID near big efforts

### Sample weeks by phase (2 gym days)

**Max-strength phase**
| Day | Session |
|---|---|
| Mon | Heavy strength A (squat/trap-bar DL ≤4RM, split squat, calf raise, anti-rotation core) |
| Tue | Easy run + 10-min proprioception/foot |
| Wed | Quality run |
| Thu | Heavy strength B (single-leg RDL, step-up, hip thrust, carries, plank/Pallof) |
| Fri | Easy + low-volume plyo (3×4 pogo/box, full recovery) |
| Sat | Long run (rolling) |
| Sun | Recovery / mobility |

**Specific / pre-race phase**
- Replace one gym day with **structured downhill reps**; gym → maintenance (2 sets, heavy, low reps).
- "Top up" RBE **every 2–3 weeks**.

### What to AVOID near big mountain long runs / races (high-confidence)
- **No novel exercises or new modalities in the final 1–2 weeks** — soreness + injury risk, no upside. (multiple coaching sources; theironstride; pointtopointstrength)
- **No hard/high-volume plyometrics** within ~72 h of a big descent block, B2B weekend, or race — DOMS lasts up to 72 h and stacks with eccentric damage. (PMC12515833)
- **No new max-strength PRs** in race week.
- **Do** keep a small neural dose in taper: e.g. drop 3×8 → **2×8 or 3×6 at same load** to retain adaptation without fatigue. (runnersconnect; theironstride)
- **Do** schedule the **single 30-min downhill RBE primer ~7 days out**, then stop damaging eccentric work. (Springer review 10.1007/s40279-020-01355-z)
- **Don't** trust low DOMS as "recovered" — MVC/RFD can stay depressed for days after descents even when soreness resolves. (PMC12592170)

## 9. Controversies / weak-evidence flags
- **RBE duration for downhill specifically is unproven** beyond ~3 weeks; the "6 months" figure is borrowed from general EIMD work. (PMC11209549)
- **DOMS reduction ≠ neuromuscular recovery** — the two dissociate. (PMC12592170)
- **Poles-while-running has zero published studies**; all pole data is from hiking/uphill walking. (CTS/Koop)
- **In-race strength-loss magnitude did not predict finish rank** in the 156 km study — but **baseline strength predicted completion**. Frame strength as a *durability/finishing* tool, not a *placing* guarantee. (PMC12832751)
- Most max-strength→economy RCTs are **road/track males**; trail extrapolation is reasonable but not directly tested.
- Primary-prevention proprioception effect has a **wide CI (NNT 33)** — strongest evidence is for athletes with prior sprains. (PMC5737043)
