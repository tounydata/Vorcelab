# Strength Training for Distance / Road Runners — Evidence Base for the Vorcelab "Renfo" Module

*Compiled 2026-06-16. Scope: peer-reviewed meta-analyses, RCTs, and reputable coaching-science sources. All effect sizes are standardized mean differences (SMD / ES, Hedges' g or Cohen's d) unless noted; negative ES for running economy (RE) = improvement (lower O₂ cost). Source URLs are inline.*

> **How to read the doses:** "%1RM" = percent of one-rep-max. RIR = reps in reserve. RPE here is the RIR-based 0–10 scale (RPE 8 = 2 RIR). Rest = inter-set recovery. All protocol tables are intended to be machine-codable into Vorcelab's deterministic engine.

---

## 0. TL;DR for the engine

- Default prescription that satisfies the largest body of evidence: **2 strength sessions/week, heavy compound lower-body lifts (≥80% 1RM, 3–6 reps, 3–4 sets, 2–3 min rest) + targeted plyometrics, 8+ weeks, progressed by RIR/double-progression.** (Balsalobre-Fernández 2016; Blagrove 2018; Llanos-Lagos 2024.)
- The headline performance number: **heavy + plyometric "combined" training improves distance-running time-trial performance with a LARGE effect (ES ≈ −1.04)**; heavy alone is moderate (ES ≈ −0.47). (Llanos-Lagos 2024.)
- The headline economy number: **pooled RE improvement ES ≈ −1.42 (large)** in highly-trained runners. (Balsalobre-Fernández 2016.)
- The headline injury number: **strength training cuts overall injury risk to ~⅓ (RR ≈ 0.32) and overuse injury roughly in half**, and it is **dose-dependent** (more volume = more protection). (Lauersen 2014, 2018.)
- **VO₂max basically does NOT change** with strength work — the gains are economy + neuromuscular + late-race durability, not aerobic capacity. (Blagrove 2018; Llanos-Lagos 2024.)

## 1. Why strength training for runners — effect sizes

### 1.1 Running economy (the primary performance lever)

| Source | Population | Finding | Effect size |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Balsalobre-Fernández et al. 2016** (J Strength Cond Res 30(8):2361) | 93 highly-trained M/LD runners (VO₂max >60) | Pooled RE improvement after strength programs | **SMD = −1.42 (95% CI −2.23 to −0.60)** — *large* |
| **Llanos-Lagos et al. 2024** (Sports Med, RE-at-speeds paper) | 195 moderately + 272 well + 185 highly trained | Heavy/high-load (≥80%1RM) | ES = −0.266 (p=0.039), *small*; **ES = −0.713 at speeds >12 km/h** |
| same | | Combined (heavy+plyo) | ES = −0.426 (p=0.018), *moderate* (best overall RE method) |
| same | | Plyometric | ES = −0.307 (p=0.028) **only at ≤12 km/h**; n.s. >12 km/h |
| same | | Submaximal load (40–79%1RM) | ES = −0.365, n.s. (p=0.131) |
| same | | Isometric | ES = −0.269, n.s. (p=0.253) |

Sources: <https://www.ingentaconnect.com/content/wk/jsc/2016/00000030/00000008/art00038> · <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11052887/> · <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-023-01978-y>

**Key codable rule (NEW/precise):** *the strength method should be matched to race speed.* Heavy lifting yields the biggest RE return at faster speeds (>12 km/h, i.e. ~<5:00/km) and in higher-VO₂max athletes; plyometrics carry the RE benefit at slower speeds (≤12 km/h). Combined work is the most robust across the 10–14.5 km/h band that covers most road racing.

### 1.2 Performance (time-trial / time-to-exhaustion)

**Llanos-Lagos et al. 2024** (*Effect of Strength Training Methods on Middle/Long-Distance Runners' Athletic Performance*, Sports Med, DOI 10.1007/s40279-024-02018-z): programs 6–40 wk, 1–4 sessions/wk.
- **Combined training: ES = −1.035 (p=0.036) — *large*** improvement on performance.
- **High-load: ES = −0.469 (p=0.029) — *moderate*.**
- Plyometric alone: ES = −0.210 (p=0.064) — n.s. for the time-trial outcome.
- **None of the methods improved VO₂max, vVO₂max, maximal MMSS, or sprint capacity (all p>0.072).** → The transfer is via economy/neuromuscular efficiency and fatigue resistance, not aerobic power.

Source: <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-024-02018-z> · <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11258194/>

**Beattie et al. 2017** (40-wk RCT, J Strength Cond Res 31(1):9–23; n=20 competitive runners): the strength group significantly improved **maximal strength (1RM squat), reactive strength (CMJ, drop-jump RSI), running economy AND velocity at VO₂max (vVO₂max)** at weeks 20 & 40, **with no hypertrophy / body-comp change** — controls changed on none. This is the longest high-quality RCT and is the best evidence that gains persist over a full season and are neural, not mass-driven.
Source: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27135468/>

**Blagrove, Howatson & Hayes 2018** (Sports Med 48(5):1117–1149; systematic review, 24 studies, 469 trained runners): strength training improved RE and neuromuscular determinants; **VO₂max and blood lactate were NOT improved but also NOT harmed.** Practical conclusion: **2–3 sessions/wk of mixed-modality strength benefits M/LD performance.**
Source: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29249083/>

### 1.3 Injury prevention

- **Lauersen, Bertelsen & Andersen 2014** (Br J Sports Med; 25 RCTs, 26,610 participants, 3,464 injuries): **strength training RR = 0.315 (95% CI 0.207–0.480)** → injuries cut to <⅓; **overuse injuries ~halved.** **Stretching had no effect (RR 0.961).** Proprioception RR 0.55; multiple-exposure RR 0.66.
  <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24100287/>
- **Lauersen, Andersen & Andersen 2018** (Br J Sports Med; "superior, dose-dependent and safe"): strength training is the single most effective category, and effect is **dose-dependent — a ~10% increase in strength-training volume was associated with a further >4-percentage-point reduction in injury risk**; higher intensity and volume both lowered risk. Programs were safe with high compliance.
  <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30131332/>

**Codable rule:** injury protection scales with *consistent dose*; a token single set is weaker than a progressively loaded program. This argues for tracking cumulative weekly strength volume in Vorcelab and rewarding adherence.

## 2. Heavy strength vs plyometrics vs explosive — protocols & when to use each

| Modality | Mechanism / adaptation | Best for | Typical dose | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Heavy / max-strength** (≥80–85% 1RM) | ↑ neural drive, motor-unit recruitment/rate coding, tendon stiffness, force/kg; minimal hypertrophy if reps low | RE at race pace >12 km/h, higher-VO₂max athletes; base of all later work | **3–4 sets × 3–6 reps @ 80–90% 1RM, 2–4 min rest, 2–3×/wk, 6–14 wk** | Llanos-Lagos 2024; Beattie 2017 |
| **Plyometric / reactive** | ↑ tendon stiffness, stretch-shortening-cycle efficiency, shorter ground contact, reactive strength index | RE at slower speeds (≤12 km/h), trail/uphill, durability; low joint-load entry point | **2–5 sets × 5–15 contacts, 1–3×/wk, 6–12 wk; quality over quantity, stop when jump height/contact quality drops** | Ramírez-Campillo; Llanos-Lagos 2024 |
| **Explosive resistance** (40–60% 1RM, max-intent velocity) | ↑ rate of force development | bridge between max-strength and plyo; in-season power maintenance | 3–5 × 3–6 @ ~40–60% 1RM, maximal concentric speed | Blagrove 2018 |
| **Combined / "complex"** (heavy lift + matched plyo) | post-activation potentiation; widest transfer | **most road runners** — best RE and performance ES | see §2.1 | Llanos-Lagos 2024 (best ES); Li 2019 RCT |

### 2.1 Reference RCT protocol (codable template) — Li et al. 2019, complex vs heavy, 8 wk, 3×/wk
*Well-trained runners, ~77 km/wk. Both lifting groups gained; complex won on reactivity & top-end RE.*

- **Complex (CT):** 3 sets of 3 paired blocks:
  1. Back squat 5 reps @ 80–85% 1RM → 4-min rest → drop jump ×6 (40 cm box)
  2. Bulgarian split squat 5/side @ 80–85% → single-leg hop ×6/side
  3. Romanian deadlift 5 @ 80–85% → double-leg hurdle hop ×6 (50 cm)
- **Heavy (HRT):** same lifts, **5×5 @ 80–85%, 3-min rest**, no plyo.
- **Results (CT / HRT):** 1RM +16.9% / +18.8%; RSI +20.0%* / +10.4%; RE@16 km/h −7.4%* / −2.9%; blood lactate@16 km/h −40.8%* / −16.1%; **5-km time −2.80%** / −2.09% (both vs control −0.07%). *= CT significantly > HRT.*

Source: <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6487184/>

**Dose-response takeaway:** as little as **8 weeks** moves the needle; ~2–3% on a 5 km is realistic and meaningful. Longer (40 wk, Beattie) keeps adding strength and RE.

## 3. Frequency & minimal effective dose

- **Volume-equated, frequency per se matters little for strength** (Grgic et al. 2018 meta-analysis): 1×/wk vs ≥3×/wk gave similar strength gains when weekly volume was matched; **lower-body strength showed no frequency advantage** (upper body slightly favored higher frequency).
  <https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-018-0149-9>
- **Building strength:** 2×/wk is the pragmatic sweet spot and is generally superior to 1×/wk when volume isn't matched; this is also what the runner meta-analyses used (Balsalobre 2×–3×/wk; Blagrove 2–3×/wk).
- **Maintaining strength (the realistic "gym ~1×/week" runner):** **1 session/week maintains strength for at least the first ~12 weeks of a season, whereas dropping to one session every 2 weeks lost ~10% of 1RM half-squat** (Spiteri-type/in-season review, Cuthbert et al. 2021). Maintenance works **if intensity is held** — keep the load heavy, just cut total sets.
  <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8363540/>

**What 1 session/week realistically achieves (codable guidance):**
- *Off-season / building phase:* 1×/wk is sub-optimal for **building** max strength but still produces meaningful injury-protection and some RE benefit; better than zero. Make that one session count: full-body, heavy compounds, 3–5 sets each of squat + hinge + calf/soleus + single-leg.
- *In-season:* 1×/wk is **sufficient to maintain** strength/power gains for ~12 weeks if load is kept heavy and intensity isn't cut — ideal for the high-mileage road runner who can't fit more.
- Practical heuristic to encode: **1×/wk = maintain; 2×/wk = build; 3×/wk = build fastest / complex training, only when running volume allows.**

## 4. Exercise selection — highest-value movements

Priority order for a road/trail runner (force the engine to cover all five buckets):

1. **Bilateral knee-dominant** — Back/front squat, leg press. Foundational max-strength, RE driver at speed.
2. **Hip-dominant / posterior chain** — Romanian/conventional deadlift, hip thrust, Nordic hamstring (the Nordic specifically is the strongest single hamstring-injury-prevention exercise in the Lauersen literature).
3. **Calf–Achilles complex, with SOLEUS emphasis (high-value, under-trained).** The **soleus produces forces up to ~6.5–8× bodyweight during running** (vs gastrocnemius ~3×) and provides >half of vertical support of the center of mass; stronger calves + stiffer Achilles correlate with better RE, and soleus-specific programs improve RE directly. Train it **bent-knee** (seated calf raise / bent-knee standing) to bias soleus, plus straight-knee for gastrocnemius. Dose: **2–3 calf exercises, 2×/wk, heavy 3×5–10.**
   <https://kinetic-revolution.com/soleus-strength-exercise-for-runners> · <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7893283/>
4. **Single-leg / unilateral** — Bulgarian split squat, step-up, single-leg RDL, single-leg calf raise. Matches running's single-support nature, exposes/【corrects asymmetries, high transfer.
5. **Anti-rotation / anti-extension trunk** — Pallof press, dead bug, side plank, suitcase carry. Running is a series of resisted-rotation single-leg stances; train the core to *resist* movement, not to crunch.

Plyometric layer (added once base strength exists): pogo hops, ankle bounces, drop jumps, bounding, single-leg hops — these load the same Achilles/soleus spring that drives economy.

## 5. Progression & autoregulation

**Double progression (default, deterministic & simple to code):**
1. Pick a rep range (e.g. 4–6).
2. Hit top of range for all sets across two sessions → **add load** (lower body +2.5–5 kg / ~2.5–5%; upper +1–2.5 kg) and drop back to bottom of range.
3. Repeat.

**RIR / RPE autoregulation (daily readiness):**
- Prescribe by RIR target, e.g. "work sets @ 2 RIR (RPE 8)."
- **Load-adjustment rule (codable):** if athlete reports RPE one point higher than prescribed (e.g. RPE 9 when 8 was the target / 1 RIR when 2 prescribed), **reduce load ~4%** for remaining sets; if easier than target, +~2–4%.
  <https://www.sportsmith.co/articles/vbt-vs-traditional-loading-finding-the-best-approach/>
- Velocity-loss alternative (if VBT available): stop a set at ~20% velocity loss from the fastest rep to cap fatigue and keep bar speed high (important for runners, who want neural quality not grind).

**Deload triggers (encode any of):**
- Planned: every 4th week, OR after 3–6 weeks of progressive loading.
- Reactive: RIR creeping down / RPE up at fixed loads (e.g. Week-3 RPE elevated → deload Week 4); bar speed dropping; sleep/HRV/running-quality decline; in-race-block weeks.
- Deload form: ~40–60% normal volume, **keep intensity** (heavy but few sets) to preserve adaptation. Never cut intensity to maintain gains.

## 6. Seasonal periodization & the strength taper

Recommended block sequence for a road runner (encode as phase states):

| Phase | Goal | Weeks | Prescription | Running context |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Anatomical Adaptation (AA)** | tissue prep, technique, work capacity | 1–6 (longer for beginners, up to ~12) | 2–3×/wk, 2–3 sets × 12–20 reps @ 40–60% 1RM, short rest; full-body | Base / general prep |
| **Max Strength** | neural force, RE driver | 4–8 | 2–3×/wk, **3–5 × 3–6 @ 80–90% 1RM, 2–4 min rest** | Build phase |
| **Power / Plyometric (Conversion)** | RFD, reactive strength, SSC | 3–6 | 2×/wk, explosive 3–5 × 3–6 @ 40–60% max velocity + plyometrics; or complex pairs | Sharpening / specific prep |
| **In-season Maintenance** | hold gains, minimize fatigue | race block | **1×/wk, heavy but low volume (2–3 × 3–5 @ ~80%) + a little plyo** | Competition |

**The strength taper (important, often done wrong):**
- **Do NOT stop heavy lifting weeks out.** Strength/power is **maintained ~4 weeks with little/no training** in trained athletes; cutting strength too early just sheds adaptation.
- Best evidence: **Berryman/concurrent-cessation study — after 8 wk concurrent training then 4 wk of strength cessation, energy cost of running, maximal power, CMJ, and 3000 m time were all MAINTAINED or even slightly improved** (3000 m: +2.40% post-training → +4.43% after 4-wk cessation; small n=8, interpret cautiously).
  <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7821917/>
- **Practical taper rule (codable):** keep strength frequency/intensity through the build; in the final ~7–10 days reduce *volume* while keeping intensity; **stop heavy lifting ~3–5 days before race day** to clear residual muscle soreness and freshen the CNS. Light, low-volume "primers" (a few explosive reps) are fine in race week.
  <https://www.outsideonline.com/health/training-performance/strength-training-taper-research/> · <https://runnersconnect.net/when-should-i-stop-strength-training-at-the-gym-before-the-marathon/>

## 7. Beginner vs advanced; female-specific notes

**Beginner vs advanced:**
- Beginners: longer AA phase, more technique, can build strength on **1–2×/wk**, fast early neural gains, lower loads. Prioritize movement competence (squat/hinge/single-leg/calf) before heavy or plyometric loading.
- Advanced/highly-trained: **bigger RE return from HEAVY work** specifically (the high-load RE effect grows with running speed and VO₂max — Llanos-Lagos 2024), need genuinely heavy loads (≥85%) and complex/plyo to keep progressing; plateaus require autoregulated progression and proper periodization.
- Across training status, **strength training never improved VO₂max** — so a high-VO₂max runner who has "maxed aerobic" gets disproportionate value from strength/economy work.

**Female-specific (evidence is thinner; flag as such):**
- **Relative adaptations to resistance training are similar between sexes**; women can/should lift heavy.
- Suggestive (lower-certainty) signal: **female runners may benefit *at least as much, possibly more*, from heavy strength training for running performance** than men. Use as a "don't under-dose women" heuristic, not a hard parameter.
- **Menstrual-cycle-based periodization is NOT supported** by current evidence: best syntheses conclude cycle-phase programming gives no reliable advantage over standard progression, and cycle phase does not appreciably change acute strength or long-term strength/hypertrophy adaptations. **Do not build cycle-phase logic into Vorcelab as an evidence-based feature.** (Offer symptom-based autoregulation instead — train to readiness.)
  <https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1054542/full>
- RED-S / low energy availability is the more important female (and male) consideration than cycle timing — strength quality and bone benefit depend on adequate fueling. (Cross-reference a separate nutrition doc.)

## 8. Controversies & weak-evidence flags

- **Mechanism of the RE benefit is not fully settled** — likely tendon stiffness + neural efficiency + altered muscle activation rather than any single cause; magnitude varies a lot between studies (wide CIs in Balsalobre −2.23 to −0.60).
- **Plyometric-alone effects on time-trial performance are non-significant** (ES −0.21, p=0.064) even though plyo helps RE at slow speeds — so plyo is an adjunct, not a standalone performance driver.
- **Taper/cessation evidence rests on small samples** (Berryman n=8); the "maintained for 4 weeks" claim is directionally robust across the strength literature but the running-specific RCTs are underpowered.
- **In-season 1×/wk maintenance** is demonstrated only to ~12 weeks; longer race seasons are under-studied.
- **Isometric and submaximal (40–79% 1RM) loading did not significantly improve RE** in the 2024 meta-analysis — deprioritize them as primary methods (isometrics still useful for tendon rehab/return-to-run).
- Most RCTs are **male, younger, and trained-but-not-elite**; extrapolation to masters, true elites, and ultra/trail specialists is partly inference.
